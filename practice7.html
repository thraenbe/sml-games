Statistical Machine Learning · Assignment 7 · Practice · Shorter Tasks, With Solutions

One big picture, eleven small problems.

Assignment 7 is really one story told twice. A fitted model is a noisy estimate — with correlated features and d ≈ n, catastrophically noisy — and the sheet offers the two standard medicines: shrink the estimate toward zero (regularization, Exercises 1–2) or average many retrainings of it (bagging, Exercise 3). Both spend a little bias to buy a lot of variance. The problems below are smaller than the sheet's, but each one carries a piece of that picture; try it before opening the solution.

Warm-ups · Exercises 1–2A. Reading coefficients

task 1weighted least squarescompute

One feature, no bias, two data points (x, y) = (1, 2) and (2, 3) with weights r = (1, 4). Minimize Σi ri(yi − wxi)². What is w⋆? How does it compare to the unweighted fit?

Show solution

Setting the derivative to zero gives w⋆ = Σrixiyi / Σrixi² = (1·1·2 + 4·2·3) / (1·1² + 4·2²) = 26/17 ≈ 1.53.

Unweighted: (2 + 6)/(1 + 4) = 1.60. The second point alone would like slope y/x = 1.5, and its weight 4 — four copies of itself — pulls the answer toward 1.5.

task 2weighted least squaresderive

Show in one line that weighted least squares on (Xi, Yi) with weights ri is ordinary least squares on rescaled data. What is the closed-form solution?

Show solution

ri(Yi − ⟨Xi, w⟩)² = (√riYi − ⟨√riXi, w⟩)² — so set X̃i = √riXi, Ỹi = √riYi and run OLS.

In matrix form, with R = diag(r): w⋆ = (XTRX)−1XTRY, unique because all ri > 0 keep XTRX positive definite when X has full column rank.

task 3partial vs marginalcompute

Standardized features with corr(X₁, X₂) = 0.5, Cov(X₁, Y) = 0.5, Cov(X₂, Y) = 1. What slope does a scatter plot of Y against X₁ suggest? What coefficient does the two-feature regression report for X₁?

Show solution

Marginal: Cov(X₁, Y)/Var(X₁) = 0.5. Partial: w₁⋆ = (c₁ − ρc₂)/(1 − ρ²) = (0.5 − 0.5·1)/(1 − 0.25) = 0.

Every bit of X₁'s association with Y is already explained by X₂ — after controlling for X₂, nothing is left. Same data, two honest answers to two different questions.

task 4partial vs marginaltrue / false

True or false? (i) If wj⋆ = 0 then Cov(Xj, Y) = 0. (ii) If Cov(Xj, Y) = 0 then wj⋆ = 0.

Show solution

Both false. (i) Take Y = X₂ with corr(X₁, X₂) = ρ ≠ 0: the regression gives w⋆ = (0, 1), yet Cov(X₁, Y) = ρ ≠ 0. (ii) Take Y = X₂ − ρX₁: then Cov(X₁, Y) = ρ − ρ = 0, yet w₁⋆ = −ρ ≠ 0.

The moral is exactly № 3: marginal covariance ignores the other features; the coefficient accounts for them.

task 5interpretationexplain

A regression on observational data reports a coefficient of −0.14 on PctUnemployed for predicting crime. A politician concludes: “lowering unemployment by one point lowers crime by 0.14.” Two reasons this is unwarranted?

Show solution

1 — not causal. The coefficient describes the conditional mean in the observed joint distribution; the claim is about an intervention. Without “no unobserved confounders, no reverse causation, true linearity” the two are different objects.

2 — not even stable. With d ≈ n and many features correlated with unemployment, the estimate has enormous variance — on the sheet's data its sign even disagrees with the marginal correlation (+0.53). A fresh sample could flip it.

task 6d ≈ nexplain

Your OLS fit with d = 99 features on n = 100 points reports in-sample R² ≈ 1.00. Great news?

Show solution

The opposite. A nearly square design matrix can fit almost any labels — including pure noise — so R² ≈ 1 signals near-interpolation, not understanding. Individual coefficients are barely identified: correlated features can trade loadings with almost no effect on the fit, so each ŵj wobbles wildly across samples. High in-sample fit and meaningless coefficients are entirely compatible.

Warm-ups · Exercises 2(f)–3B. Variance, and the two medicines

task 7bias–variancecompute

An estimator of wj⋆ = 1 has mean 0.27 and variance 0.12 (the sheet's lasso near λ* ≈ 0.03). Its expected squared error? Compare with the near-unregularized version: mean 0.17, variance 0.70.

Show solution

MSE = bias² + variance = (0.27 − 1)² + 0.12 = 0.533 + 0.12 ≈ 0.65. Unregularized: (0.17 − 1)² + 0.70 = 0.689 + 0.70 ≈ 1.39 — worse on both terms here.

The regularized estimator is more biased in exchange for stability, and the total ledger favors it — that is the bias–variance tradeoff in two numbers.

task 8bagging, ideal casecompute

You average B = 25 predictors trained on independent samples; each has variance σ² = 0.5 at your test point (and common mean). Variance of the average? What fraction of the single-model variance did averaging remove?

Show solution

Var(hB) = σ²/B = 0.5/25 = 0.02. Removed: (1 − 1/B)σ² = 0.48, i.e. 96%.

Note what did not change: the mean is still f̄, so the bias² term of the error is exactly what it was for one model. Averaging is a variance-only instrument.

task 9the correlation floorcompute

Now the B predictors come from bootstrap resamples of one dataset: pairwise correlation ρ = 0.4, individual variance σ²D = 0.9. Compute Var(hB | D) for B = 10 and for B → ∞.

Show solution

Var = (ρ + (1 − ρ)/B)σ²D. At B = 10: (0.4 + 0.6/10)·0.9 = 0.46·0.9 ≈ 0.41. As B → ∞: ρσ²D = 0.4·0.9 = 0.36.

Ten models already sit within 15% of the infinite-forest limit — once predictors are correlated, extra copies buy almost nothing. The floor, not the budget, is the binding constraint.

task 10random forestsexplain

Using Var(hB | D) = (ρ + (1 − ρ)/B)σ²D, explain in two sentences why random forests restrict each split to a random subset of features.

Show solution

Trees grown on bootstrap copies of the same data pick the same few strong features at their top splits, which makes ρ large — and the formula says no amount of averaging can push the variance below ρσ²D. Randomizing the candidate features forces trees to grow along different variables, decorrelating them; a smaller ρ lowers the B → ∞ floor itself, which is worth more than any number of extra correlated trees.

task 11bootstrap fine printexplain

The sheet estimates the mean and variance of ŵj from T = 200 bootstrap refits. What are these two numbers trying to approximate, and in which direction are they biased?

Show solution

They approximate the estimator's true sampling distribution — its mean and variance under independent fresh samples of n points from the population.

But all 200 resamples are drawn from a single dataset and any two share ≈ 63% of their rows, so they are far from independent — the bootstrap variance typically underestimates the true sampling variance. (The same ≈ 63% overlap is what makes ρ > 0 in № 9: it is one phenomenon wearing two hats.)

If you remember one thing: every number a fitted model reports is a random variable. Exercises 1–2 ask what its mean is aiming at (a partial, non-causal effect) and show its variance exploding at d ≈ n; Exercise 3 shows the two levers you own — shrink the estimate (λ) or average retrainings (B) — and proves each lever's limit: shrinkage costs bias, and averaging stops at the correlation floor ρσ².
