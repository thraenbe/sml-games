Statistical Machine Learning · Bayesian Decision Theory · Mock Exam Q3, Expanded

Not every mistake costs the same.

The mock exam's Question 3 packs the whole of Bayesian decision theory into one binary world: a prior π = ℙ(Y=1), two class-conditionals, and a loss where a missed positive costs a but a false alarm costs 1. Everything — the posterior η(x), the classifier f°, the Bayes error, the shifted threshold, the tipping prior — falls out of one comparison. This page builds that comparison three times: as areas, as risk lines, and as a prior dial.

class Y = 1 · predict 1 class Y = 0 · predict 0 thresholds & tipping points

The setup · exam notationOne prior, two conditionals, four areas

The world: X ∈ {0, 1}, Y ∈ {0, 1}, prior ℙ(Y=1) = 2/5, and class-conditionals

ℙ(X=1 | Y=1) = 4/5,    ℙ(X=1 | Y=0) = 1/5

Read: positives usually show the signal, negatives usually don't. Every quantity in the question comes from the joint, and the joint is just prior × conditional — four multiplications:

ℙ(X=1, Y=1) = 2⁄5 · 4⁄5 = 8⁄25  ·  ℙ(X=0, Y=1) = 2⁄25  ·  ℙ(X=1, Y=0) = 3⁄25  ·  ℙ(X=0, Y=0) = 12⁄25

From it, the marginal μ(x) = ℙ(X=x) sums each column: μ(1) = 8/25 + 3/25 = 11/25, μ(0) = 14/25. And the posterior η(x) = ℙ(Y=1 | X=x) divides the positive part by the column total:

η(1) = (8/25) / (11/25) = 8/11,    η(0) = (2/25) / (14/25) = 1/7

That is all Bayes' rule is: an area ratio. The figure draws the joint as a unit square — width split by the prior, heights by the conditionals — so you can literally see μ and η:

Part (a) · the 0–1 baselineThe Bayes classifier f° and its error

Under 0–1 loss, predict the majority side of each column: f°(x) = 1 iff η(x) ≥ ½. Here η(1) = 8/11 > ½ and η(0) = 1/7 < ½, so f°(1) = 1, f°(0) = 0 — follow the signal. The Bayes error collects the losing share of each column, weighted by how often the column occurs:

ℙ(f°(X) ≠ Y) = Σx μ(x) · min(η(x), 1 − η(x)) = 11⁄25 · 3⁄11 + 14⁄25 · 1⁄7 = 1⁄5

No classifier of any kind beats 1/5 in this world — the overlap of the two classes makes that error unavoidable. Every learning-theory bound on the sheets measures distance to exactly this number.

Part (b) · costs enterTwo risks, one comparison, a moving threshold

Now the exam's asymmetric loss: ℓ(y=1, ŷ=0) = a (a missed positive), ℓ(y=0, ŷ=1) = 1 (a false alarm), correct predictions free. At a given x you know η(x), and each action has a conditional risk — its expected loss:

R(1 | x) = 1 · (1 − η(x))   ·   R(0 | x) = a · η(x)

Predicting 1 can only go wrong if the truth is 0 (probability 1 − η, cost 1); predicting 0 can only go wrong if the truth is 1 (probability η, cost a). The Bayes decision takes the cheaper side:

predict 1  ⟺  1 − η(x) < a η(x)  ⟺  η(x) > 1 / (1 + a)

At a = 1 this is the familiar ½. As misses get pricier the threshold slides down: you demand less and less evidence before sounding the alarm. At η = 1/(1+a) both actions cost the same, so either tie-break is optimal — the exam says so explicitly. Watch the machine:

Part (c) · the prior dialKeep the loss, move the world

Part (c) flips the experiment: keep the standard 0–1 loss and the class-conditionals ℙ(X=0 | Y=1) = 1/5, ℙ(X=0 | Y=0) = 4/5, but treat the prior π = ℙ(Y=1) as adjustable. The posterior at x = 0 becomes a function of π — the exam's own display:

η(0) = 1⁄5 π  /  ( 1⁄5 π + 4⁄5 (1 − π) ) = π / (4 − 3π)

Breaking ties toward 1, the decision at x = 0 is 1 iff η(0) ≥ ½ ⟺ π ≥ 4(1 − π) ⟺ π ≥ 4/5 — the smallest such prior is π = 4/5. The intuition: x = 0 is evidence against class 1 (it is four times likelier under Y=0), so the prior must be lopsided enough to overrule the evidence. The same algebra with a general threshold gives both tipping priors at once — for η(x) ≥ 1/(1+a):

π0* = 4 / (4 + a)   ·   π1* = 1 / (1 + 4a)

(a = 1 recovers 4/5 and 1/5.) Below π₁*, even the positive signal can't rescue class 1; above π₀*, even the negative signal can't stop it. In between is the only regime where the feature actually decides.

The synthesisCosts and priors are the same dial

Move the threshold (part b)

Fix the world, change the loss: the posteriors η(x) stay put and the threshold 1/(1+a) slides. A decision flips when the threshold crosses a posterior — at a = 6 for the exam's x = 0.

Move the posteriors (part c)

Fix the loss, change the world: the threshold stays at ½ and the dots slide along η(0) = π/(4−3π), η(1) = 4π/(1+3π). The same flip happens when a posterior crosses the threshold — at π = 4/5.

Both dials end at the identical inequality a η(x) ≷ 1 − η(x), which is why exam part (b) and part (c) are one derivation wearing two costumes. In likelihood-ratio form: predict 1 iff ℙ(X=x|Y=1) / ℙ(X=x|Y=0) > (1−π) / (a π) — evidence on the left, prior and costs bundled on the right.

The bigger picture. Bayesian decision theory is the course's definition of “best”: f° is the target every consistency statement, excess-risk bound, and surrogate loss on the sheets is measured against. And its whole machinery is one comparison of expected costs, a η versus 1 − η. Costs tilt the comparison, priors move η, and the feature only matters in the window where the two forces don't already settle the argument. When the exam asks for “the smallest π” or “the decision rule in terms of η”, it is asking you to run this one comparison — the arithmetic before it (joint → marginal → posterior) is three multiplications and a division, drawn as areas in Figure 1.

The exam question · a full-marks answerQuestion 3, answered as you'd write it

Here is the complete task and a solution at full-marks level — nothing more than the rubric pays for, nothing less. Note how short it is once the machine above is internalized: the whole answer is one joint table, one risk comparison, and one inequality in π.

The task · 5 points≈12 min · medium · Bayesian decision theory

Let X ∈ {0, 1} and Y ∈ {0, 1} with class prior ℙ(Y=1) = 2/5, ℙ(Y=0) = 3/5 and class-conditionals ℙ(X=1 | Y=1) = 4/5, ℙ(X=1 | Y=0) = 1/5.

(a) (2 pt) Compute the marginal μ(x) = ℙ(X=x) and η(x) = ℙ(Y=1 | X=x) for x ∈ {0, 1}. State the 0–1 Bayes classifier f° and its Bayes error ℙ(f°(X) ≠ Y).
(b) (1.5 pt) Now use the asymmetric loss with ℓ(y=1, ŷ=0) = a, ℓ(y=0, ŷ=1) = 1 (correct predictions costing 0). Derive the Bayes decision rule in terms of η(x) (assume a > 0).
(c) (1.5 pt) Keep the standard 0–1 loss and the fixed class-conditionals, but treat the prior π = ℙ(Y=1) as adjustable. Find the smallest π for which the Bayes decision at x = 0 is 1 (breaking ties toward 1).

Full-marks answer5 / 5 pt

(a) Joint = prior × conditional: ℙ(X=1, Y=1) = 2⁄5 · 4⁄5 = 8⁄25, ℙ(X=0, Y=1) = 2⁄25, ℙ(X=1, Y=0) = 3⁄25, ℙ(X=0, Y=0) = 12⁄25. Hence μ(1) = 11⁄25, μ(0) = 14⁄25 and η(1) = (8/25)/(11/25) = 8⁄11, η(0) = (2/25)/(14/25) = 1⁄7 +1 pt. Since η(1) > ½ and η(0) < ½: f°(1) = 1, f°(0) = 0 +0.5 pt. Bayes error = Σx μ(x) min(η(x), 1 − η(x)) = 11⁄25 · 3⁄11 + 14⁄25 · 1⁄7 = 1⁄5 +0.5 pt.

(b) Conditional risks: R(1 | x) = 1 · (1 − η(x)), R(0 | x) = a · η(x). Predict 1 iff 1 − η(x) < a η(x) ⟺ η(x) > 1/(1 + a) +1.5 pt. (At η = 1/(1+a) both actions have equal risk, so either tie-break is optimal.)

(c) With ℙ(X=0 | Y=1) = 1/5 and ℙ(X=0 | Y=0) = 4/5 fixed, the posterior at x = 0 is η(0) = 1⁄5 π / (1⁄5 π + 4⁄5(1 − π)) +0.5 pt. Breaking ties toward 1, the decision at x = 0 is 1 iff η(0) ≥ ½ ⟺ 1⁄5 π ≥ 4⁄5(1 − π) ⟺ π ≥ 4(1 − π) ⟺ π ≥ 4⁄5. The smallest such prior is π = 4⁄5 +1 pt.

Practice · same machine, new numbersTwo more, before the real exam

Both tasks run on the identical recipe — joint → marginal → posterior → compare risks. Do them on paper first; each hides its full solution below.

Practice 1the timid prior

A spam filter: Y = 1 means spam, and X ∈ {0, 1} flags a suspicious link. Prior ℙ(Y=1) = 1/5, class-conditionals ℙ(X=1 | Y=1) = 3/5, ℙ(X=1 | Y=0) = 1/5.

(a) Compute μ(x) and η(x) for x ∈ {0, 1}, state the 0–1 Bayes classifier f° and its Bayes error. Something unusual happens — say what.
(b) Using the loss ℓ(y=1, ŷ=0) = a, ℓ(y=0, ŷ=1) = 1 and breaking ties toward 1, find the smallest miss cost a for which the decision at x = 1 becomes 1.

show solution

(a) Joint: ℙ(1,1) = 1⁄5 · 3⁄5 = 3⁄25, ℙ(0,1) = 2⁄25, ℙ(1,0) = 4⁄5 · 1⁄5 = 4⁄25, ℙ(0,0) = 16⁄25. So μ(1) = 7⁄25, μ(0) = 18⁄25, and η(1) = (3/25)/(7/25) = 3⁄7, η(0) = (2/25)/(18/25) = 1⁄9.

The unusual part: η(1) = 3/7 < ½ too — so f°(1) = 0 and f°(0) = 0. The Bayes classifier is constant: the link flag is genuine evidence (3/7 ≫ the prior 1/5), but not enough to clear ½, so the optimal 0–1 rule ignores X entirely. Bayes error = 7⁄25 · 3⁄7 + 18⁄25 · 1⁄9 = 3⁄25 + 2⁄25 = 1⁄5 — it simply pays the whole spam mass ℙ(Y=1) = 1/5, as a constant-0 rule must.

(b) By part (b) of the exam, predict 1 at x = 1 (ties toward 1) iff η(1) ≥ 1/(1 + a) ⟺ 3⁄7 ≥ 1/(1 + a) ⟺ 3(1 + a) ≥ 7 ⟺ a ≥ 4⁄3. The smallest miss cost is a = 4/3: price a missed spam just a third more than a false alarm and the flag starts to matter again. (Check it in Figure 2's machine: the threshold must drop below 3/7.)

Practice 2the tipping prior, re-run

Keep the standard 0–1 loss and fix the class-conditionals ℙ(X=1 | Y=1) = 3/4, ℙ(X=1 | Y=0) = 1/4; the prior π = ℙ(Y=1) is adjustable and ties break toward 1.

(a) Find the smallest π for which the Bayes decision at x = 0 is 1.
(b) Repeat with the asymmetric loss ℓ(y=1, ŷ=0) = 3, ℓ(y=0, ŷ=1) = 1. Compare the two answers in one sentence.

show solution

(a) With ℙ(X=0 | Y=1) = 1/4 and ℙ(X=0 | Y=0) = 3/4, the posterior at x = 0 is η(0) = 1⁄4 π / (1⁄4 π + 3⁄4(1 − π)) = π / (3 − 2π). The decision at x = 0 is 1 iff η(0) ≥ ½ ⟺ 2π ≥ 3 − 2π ⟺ π ≥ 3⁄4. Smallest prior: π = 3/4.

(b) Now the threshold is 1/(1 + a) = ¼: π / (3 − 2π) ≥ ¼ ⟺ 4π ≥ 3 − 2π ⟺ 6π ≥ 3 ⟺ π ≥ 1⁄2. Smallest prior: π = 1/2.

Comparison: tripling the miss cost does the same job as a much heavier prior — the required π drops from 3/4 to 1/2, because costs and priors enter the decision only through the single product a η ≷ 1 − η. That is the synthesis section in one line.
