Statistical Machine Learning · Assignment 7 · Exercise 3 · Why Bagging Reduces Variance

Averaging kills variance. Correlation sets the floor.

A single learner's expected test error splits into three parts — bias², variance, noise — and exactly one of them can be averaged away. Train B independent copies and the variance divides by B; that is bagging's entire trick. But bootstrap copies of one dataset are never independent, and the exercise's final formula says precisely what survives the averaging: ρσ². Random forests exist to attack that ρ.

single predictors A(Sb) variance — what averaging shrinks bias & the floor — what it can't touch

Part (a) · the decompositionThree sources of error, one identity

Fix a test point x. Train a learner A on a random sample S, and let f̄(x) = 𝔼S[A(S)(x)] be the average predictor — what you'd get by training on every possible dataset and averaging — while f*(x) = 𝔼[y | x] is the best possible prediction. Expanding the squared error and watching all three cross terms vanish (each contains a factor with mean zero, and S is independent of the fresh test response) gives the identity:

𝔼S,y|x[(A(S)(x) − y)²] = (f̄(x) − f*(x))² + VarS(A(S)(x)) + Var(y | x) bias² · variance · noise

Each term has its own personality. Bias is a property of the model class: even the average of infinitely many fits misses f* if the class is too rigid. Variance is a property of the estimation: the fit wobbles from sample to sample. Noise belongs to the world and no model touches it. Bagging is a surgical strike on the middle term only.

Part (b) · the ideal bagged predictorRemove the randomness, remove the variance

Define hbag(x) = 𝔼S[A(S)(x)] = f̄(x) — the average predictor itself. It is a fixed function, not random over S, so when we compute its expected loss the variance term simply isn't there:

𝔼y|x[(hbag(x) − y)²] = (f̄(x) − f*(x))² + Var(y | x)   ≤   the single-model error

The gap between the two sides is exactly VarS(A(S)(x)) ≥ 0. Averaging can only help — and what it removes is precisely the variance term, all of it. The catch: hbag needs the average over all datasets, which nobody has. The rest of the exercise is about approximating it with finitely many.

Parts (c)–(e) · independent samplesVariance ÷ B, bias untouched

Suppose we could draw B genuinely independent training samples and average the resulting predictors: hB(x) = (1/B) Σb Fb. Linearity of expectation keeps the mean at f̄(x), and independence makes all covariances vanish, so the variance of the average is (1/B²) · Bσ² :

𝔼[hB(x)] = f̄(x)   and   Var(hB(x)) = σ²(x) / B

Plugging hB into the part (a) decomposition gives its expected loss as bias² + σ²/B + noise — the improvement over one model is exactly (1 − 1/B) σ²(x): at B = 25, some 96% of the variance term is gone. And that is all that is gone.

(e) · which base learners should you bag?

Averaging divides variance by B but leaves bias² exactly alone — so bagging pays off for low-bias, high-variance learners. The textbook example is a deep, unpruned decision tree: flexible enough to be nearly unbiased on any reasonable target, but wildly different from resample to resample — which is precisely the part averaging repairs. Bagging a heavily biased learner just averages many copies of the same mistake.

Parts (f) & (g) · the realistic caseBootstrap copies are correlated — and it shows

In practice there is one dataset D, and the B predictors are trained on bootstrap resamples of it — any two resamples share about 63% of their rows, so the predictors are correlated. Part (f)'s variance identity Var(ΣFb) = Σ Var(Fb) + 2 Σb<b' Cov(Fb, Fb') now has B(B − 1)/2 nonzero covariance pairs, each equal to ρD(x) σ²D(x) by the definition of correlation. Counting terms and dividing by B²:

Var( (1/B) Σb Fb | D ) = ( ρD(x) + (1 − ρD(x)) / B ) σ²D(x)

Read it as a partition of the variance into a defeatable part, which dies like 1/B, and an undefeatable part ρσ², which no amount of averaging touches. Set ρ = 0 and the ideal 1/B law reappears; set ρ = 1 and averaging does nothing at all — you trained the same model B times.

Part (h) · random forestsDon't add trees — subtract agreement

As B → ∞ the variance converges to ρD(x) σ²D(x) — a hard floor. Trees grown on bootstrap copies of the same dataset are highly correlated: they tend to pick the same few strong features for their top splits, so they make the same mistakes together, and averaging correlated mistakes doesn't cancel them. Random forests attack ρ directly: at every split, only a random subset of features competes. Different trees are forced to build along different variables, ρD(x) drops, and the floor sinks. Omit that step and no amount of extra trees pushes the variance below ρσ² — the formula from part (g) says so.

The bigger picture. Bagging is a pure variance instrument: it cannot fix bias, cannot reduce noise, and against variance it wields two very different terms. The 1/B term is free — just train more models. The ρ term is structural — you lower it only by making the models genuinely disagree: different feature subsets, different subsamples, different randomness. That is why a random forest is not "many trees" but many decorrelated trees, and why this exercise and Exercises 1–2 are one story: regularization and averaging are the two standard ways of paying a little bias (or a little honesty about it) to make variance affordable.
