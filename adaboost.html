Statistical Machine Learning · Assignment 8 · Exercise 1 · Boosting Decision Stumps

Wrong answers get louder.

AdaBoost builds a strong classifier out of laughably weak ones — decision stumps, rules that ask a single yes/no question about a single coordinate. The whole mechanism fits on the smallest dataset that can show it: four positive points at the corners of a square, one negative point at its centre. This page runs the algorithm by hand on those five points, then reads off the general law hiding in them: mistakes get reweighted, the vote gets richer, and the training error dies exponentially fast.

class y = +1 — the four corners class y = −1 — the centre the data weights Di

the algorithm · one breathA weighted vote, grown one round at a time

AdaBoost keeps a distribution D(t) over the training points (initially uniform, 1/n each). Each round t: train a weak classifier ht on the weighted data and record its weighted error εt = Σmistakes Di(t); give it a say in the vote; then multiply every point's weight by e−wtyiht(xi) and renormalize — mistakes get louder, solved points get quieter. The final classifier is

h(x) = sign( Σt wt ht(x) ) , wt = ½ ln ( (1 − εt) / εt )

A decision stump hj,θ,s predicts s where xj > θ and −s elsewhere — one axis-aligned line, two labelled half-planes. Slide the threshold past all the points and the stump degenerates into the constant classifier. Keep that degenerate case in mind: it wins round one.

part (a) · round oneThe best stump doesn't split at all

Any genuine split misclassifies at least two of the four corners: whichever side of the line carries the prediction −1 contains at least two positives (and if it contains the centre too, flipping s just moves the damage to the other side). So every splitting stump has weighted error ≥ 2/5 — while the constant rule h₁ ≡ +1 errs only on the centre: ε₁ = 1/5, giving w₁ = ½ ln 4 ≈ 0.69. Verify it yourself below, then apply the reweighting and watch the game change: under the new weights the constant rule is worth exactly a coin flip (ε = ½), and a genuine stump takes the lead.

The reweighting numbers are worth doing once by hand. With ε₁ = 1/5 the update multiplies the misclassified centre by ew₁ = 2 and each correct corner by e−w₁ = ½; renormalizing gives centre ½, corners ⅛. Notice what the update achieved: the misclassified mass is now exactly one half — on the new weights, h₁ has been reduced to random guessing. That is not a coincidence of this dataset; it is an identity of AdaBoost, and it is the reason the next round is forced to find something genuinely new.

parts (b) & (c) · how many rounds?Three stumps, because + | − | + takes three

The five points demand a non-monotone pattern along x₁: positive, then negative, then positive. One stump draws one line — hopeless. Two stumps can't do it either (that is part (c): a vote sign(w₁h₁ + w₂h₂) that labels the centre −1 must let some stump predict −1 there, and any axis-aligned stump that does so also swallows two far-side corners; chasing the signs through both cases shows the four corners can never all come out +1). Three classifiers suffice: the constant rule plus two thresholds on the same coordinate, at θ = 0.25 and θ = 0.75. Toggle them below.

parts (d) & (e) · the general lawWeak on purpose, and provably fast

(d) · why weak learners?

A strong learner could fit (and overfit) the data in round one, leaving boosting nothing to combine — the ensemble inherits its full complexity immediately. Ensemble complexity grows every round, so starting from weak, low-complexity rules keeps the final classifier as simple as the task allows. Weakness is not a bug being tolerated; it is the resource being spent gradually.

(e) · the exponential clock

If every round's weighted error stays below ½ − γ, the lecture's bound says the training error of the vote is at most e−2γ²T. Training error lives on the grid 0, 1/n, 2/n, … — so the instant the bound drops below 1/n, the error is exactly 0.

training error ≤ e−2γ²T < 1/n  ⟺  T > ln n / 2γ²

Read the formula the optimistic way: T grows only logarithmically in the sample size. A million training points and a pathetic edge of γ = 0.05 — classifiers barely better than coin flips — still reach zero training error in 2764 rounds. Slide the two dials and watch the crossing point.

The bigger picture. AdaBoost is a bookkeeping device for one quantity: the average exponential loss of the running vote. The data weights are that ledger — a point's weight is proportional to e−yift(xi), so attention concentrates exactly where the ensemble is wrong or barely right. Each round's wt is the choice that shrinks the ledger the most, and it leaves the round's own classifier reduced to a coin flip on the new weights — forcing genuine novelty next round. Five points already show the whole ritual: a degenerate stump wins, its one mistake gets loud, three rounds carve + | − | +, and in general the training error dies like e−2γ²T. What happens if you keep boosting long after zero is Exercise 2's story — and the whole algorithm re-appears as gradient descent on a loss in Exercise 3.
