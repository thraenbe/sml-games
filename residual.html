Statistical Machine Learning · Exercise 1 · Linear Regression & Orthogonality

The residual is what the model leaves behind

Least squares has a purely geometric heart: the best weights are exactly the ones whose error vector stands perpendicular to everything the model can produce. This page walks the exercise's five parts with two pictures you can move.

what the model can produce — X​v, image(X) the residual r — the error left over

Setup · the definitionWhat the residual is

We have n data points and d features: X ∈ ℝn×d holds one data point per row, Y ∈ ℝn holds the true values, and a weight vector w ∈ ℝd makes the predictions Xw. The residual is

r = Y − Xw ∈ ℝn

Read it one coordinate at a time: ri = Yi − ⟨xi, w⟩ is truth minus prediction at data point i. Positive means the model under-shoots, negative means it over-shoots, zero means a perfect fit there. The least-squares loss is nothing but the squared length of this vector, ‖Xw − Y‖² = ‖r‖², so minimizing the loss means making the residual as short as possible. Every candidate w has its own residual; the exercise asks which one is shortest.

Part (a) · the algebraPerturb the weights, get a parabola

Everything follows from one expansion. Move the weights from w to w + t v: the new residual is r − t Xv, and its squared length is

‖r − tXv‖² = ‖r‖² − 2t⟨r, Xv⟩ + t²‖Xv‖²

As a function of the step size t, the loss along any direction v is an upward parabola — exactly the shape in figure 1. The whole characterization of optimality lives in the middle term: the coefficient ⟨r, Xv⟩ is the parabola's slope at t = 0.

Parts (b) & (c) · the equivalenceMinimizer ⟺ residual ⊥ everything the model can produce

(b) minimizer ⇒ orthogonal

Suppose ⟨r, Xv⟩ ≠ 0 for some direction v. Then the parabola has nonzero slope at t = 0, so stepping to its vertex, t = ⟨r, Xv⟩ / ‖Xv‖², strictly lowers the loss by ⟨r, Xv⟩² / ‖Xv‖². A minimizer admits no such improvement — so at a minimizer every ⟨r, Xv⟩ must vanish.

(c) orthogonal ⇒ minimizer

Conversely, if ⟨r, Xv⟩ = 0 for every v, the middle term dies and part (a) with t = 1 becomes Pythagoras: ‖r − Xv‖² = ‖r‖² + ‖Xv‖² ≥ ‖r‖². Every competing weight vector w + v does at least as badly, so w is a minimizer. Figure 2 lets you feel this inequality.

Part (d) · the computationOrthogonality is the normal equations

The abstract condition compresses into one linear system. Since ⟨r, Xv⟩ = (XTr)Tv, asking it to vanish for every v is asking the vector XTr to be zero:

XT(Y − Xw) = 0   ⟺   XTX w = XTY

These are the normal equations — “normal” meaning perpendicular, not ordinary. In figure 1 this is the readout ⟨r, x⟩ hitting zero: with d = 1 the system is a single equation, and solving it gives the snap-to ŵ = ⟨x, y⟩ / ‖x‖².

Part (e) · the picturePrediction as a shadow, residual as the plumb line

The vectors Xv for all v ∈ ℝd form a subspace of ℝn: image(X), everything the model could possibly predict. Orthogonality to each Xv says r ∈ image(X)⊥. So at the optimum the data splits into two perpendicular pieces:

Y = X ŵ + r,    X ŵ ∈ image(X),   r ⊥ image(X)

That is the orthogonal decomposition of Y: the prediction Xŵ is the orthogonal projection of Y onto image(X) — its shadow on the plane of everything expressible — and the residual is the plumb line from Y down to that shadow. Perpendicular precisely because that is the shortest connection.

The one-line summary. The residual r = Y − Xw is the part of the data the model fails to explain — and w is optimal exactly when that unexplained part is orthogonal to everything the model can explain. Calculus (∇‖Xw − Y‖² = 0), algebra (XTXw = XTY), and geometry (Xŵ = projimage(X)Y) are three phrasings of this single fact.
