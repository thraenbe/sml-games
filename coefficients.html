Statistical Machine Learning · Assignment 7 · Exercises 1 & 2 · Weights & What Coefficients Mean

A coefficient is not a correlation.

These two exercises look like bookkeeping — reweight a loss, read off a fitted number — but together they ask the sheet's real question: what does ŵj actually tell you? The answer is a partial, statistical, high-variance statement about your one sample, and each of those three adjectives is a warning. This page works through the warnings in order, ending at the λ dial where bias is traded for variance on purpose.

marginal — one feature looked at alone partial — after the other features speak the regularization dial λ

Exercise 1 · the warm-upWeighted least squares: the same machine, reweighted evidence

Give each data point a weight ri > 0 and minimize the reweighted empirical error:

L(w) = (1/n) Σi ri (Yi − ⟨Xi, w⟩)²

With R = diag(r₁, …, rn) this is (1/n)(Y − Xw)TR(Y − Xw). Setting the gradient (2/n)XTR(Xw − Y) to zero gives reweighted normal equations, and since all ri > 0 make XTRX positive definite (for full-column-rank X), the minimizer is unique:

w⋆ = (XTRX)−1XTRY

The tidy way to remember it: weighting is rescaling. Replace each row by √ri Xi and √ri Yi and run ordinary least squares — a point with weight 4 counts exactly like 4 copies of itself. The machine doesn't care why the evidence is weighted; it just balances whatever evidence it is handed. That indifference is the point: everything that follows is about what the balanced answer means.

Exercise 2 (a) · Frisch–Waugh–LovellWhat ŵj measures: the partial effect

For a single centred feature the story is simple: the optimal slope is Cov(X, Y) / Var(X) — a rescaled correlation. With two standardized features whose correlation is ρ, solving the normal equations XTXw = XTY gives something different:

w₁⋆ = ( Cov(X₁, Y) − ρ Cov(X₂, Y) ) / (1 − ρ²)

The numerator subtracts the part of X₁'s association that X₂ already accounts for. Define the residuals of X₁ and Y after their best linear predictions from X₂ — written X₁⊥ and Y⊥ — and the formula collapses back into the familiar Cov/Var shape, applied to leftovers:

wj⋆ = Cov(Xj⊥, Y⊥) / Var(Xj⊥)

That is the meaning of a regression coefficient: the linear association between Xj and Y after removing what the other features already explain — the partial effect of Xj given the rest. The general case (d > 2) is the same computation with block-matrix algebra. Play with the three ingredients below and watch the marginal and partial views come apart.

Exercise 2 (b) & (c) · two warningsZero is not “unrelated”, and 0.5 is not a lever

(b) · neither direction holds

wj⋆ = 0 does not imply Cov(Xj, Y) = 0, and Cov = 0 does not imply wj⋆ = 0 — both presets in Figure 2 are counterexamples. Marginal covariance ignores the other features; the coefficient accounts for them. The two numbers answer different questions, so neither can stand in for the other.

(c) · not causal

“Increasing Xj by one unit increases Y by 0.5” is a claim about an intervention. The coefficient only describes the conditional mean inside the joint distribution you observed. The two coincide under assumptions — no unobserved confounders, no reverse causation, true linearity — that essentially never hold in observational data. Regression describes; it does not promise.

Exercise 2 (d) & (e) · Communities & Crime99 features, 100 communities: the estimate falls apart

Now the warnings become numbers. On the Communities & Crime data (d = 99 standardized features, a subsample of n = 100 communities, target: violent-crime rate, feature of interest: PctUnemployed), OLS reports an in-sample R² ≈ 1.00 — with a nearly square design matrix it can fit the training labels almost perfectly, which is a red flag, not an achievement.

(d) · OLS, no regularization

marginal correlation ≈ +0.53
coefficient ŵjOLS ≈ −0.14

Even the sign flips. Unemployment has many highly correlated competitors — poverty, low income, low education, single-parent households — and swapping one for another barely changes the fit, so the optimizer is free to give unemployment a negative loading that other coordinates cancel out. The estimate has enormous variance: a different sample of 100 communities would give a wildly different number.

(e) · lasso, λ = 0.3

ŵjLasso = 0  ·  only 6 of 99 coefficients survive

The opposite failure mode: a reader might now conclude unemployment has no association with crime — also wrong, since the +0.53 marginal correlation hasn't gone anywhere. Among correlated variables the lasso solution is not unique in spirit: it picks one representative per cluster, and a slightly different sample or λ would crown a different feature.

So the honest summary of (d)–(e) is symmetric: the unregularized number is meaningless because its variance is huge, and the regularized zero is meaningful only as “not selected”, never as “unrelated”.

Exercise 2 (f) & (g) · the bootstrap experimentTurning the λ dial: bias buys variance

To watch the trade-off directly, the sheet builds a synthetic target on the same design matrix where the truth is known: a sparse w⋆ with 10 active coordinates and wj⋆ = 1 on the feature of interest. Then: draw T = 200 bootstrap samples, fit a lasso at every λ on a log grid, and record the bootstrap mean and variance of ŵj. Those two curves are exactly the two ingredients of the bias–variance decomposition.

The deviation of the mean curve from wj⋆ = 1 is the (estimated) bias of ŵj, and the right panel is its variance — so Figure 3 is the classical bias–variance decomposition, observed in the wild for ℓ¹ regularization: λ shifts probability mass between the two terms, and the sweet spot λ* ≈ 0.03 sits where the mean is closest to the truth while the variance has already dropped by about a factor of six.

Part (g) adds the fine print: the bootstrap mean and variance are trying to approximate the estimator's true sampling distribution — what would happen under independent fresh samples of 100 communities. But all 200 resamples are drawn from one dataset and any two of them share ≈ 63% of their rows, so they are far from independent, and the bootstrap variance typically underestimates the real sampling variance. Even the honest-looking error bars are optimistic. Keep that ≈ 63% overlap in mind — it returns as the villain ρ in Exercise 3.

The bigger picture. A regression coefficient answers one precise question: how the conditional mean of Y moves with Xj after the other features have explained what they can, in the sample you happened to draw. It is not a marginal correlation — the sign can differ. It is not a causal effect — no intervention was observed. And when d ≈ n with correlated features, it is barely an estimate at all. Regularization doesn't restore the naive reading; it deliberately biases the estimate toward 0 to buy enough variance reduction that the number stabilizes. Managing that budget — bias bought, variance sold — is the actual subject of this sheet, and Exercise 3 plays the same trade with a different instrument: averaging.
