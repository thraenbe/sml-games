Statistical Machine Learning · Foundations · The Matrix Toolkit

A matrix is not a table of numbers.

It is a machine that moves vectors — and every headline formula in this course is a short chain of the same few machines: Xw, XTX, (XTX)−1XTy, X(XTX)−1XT, K−1y. This page builds the moves one at a time, each with a worked 2×2 example, and ends where the sheets begin.

columns — where the machine sends the basis rows — one dot product per data point det & eigenvalues — the machine's dials

The setupData becomes a matrix

An n × d matrix has n rows and d columns; entry Aij sits in row i, column j. In this course one convention does almost all the work: the design matrix X ∈ ℝn×d stacks the n data points as rows, one column per feature. Three houses described by two features (area, age):

X = 901212036041   — row i is house i, column j is feature j

Adding matrices and scaling them works entry by entry, and only same-shaped matrices can be added:

1201 + 3012 = 4213  ·  2 · 1201 = 2402

That part really is bookkeeping. The first real move is multiplication — and it is worth learning twice.

Move 0 · the dot productThree vector facts the proofs live on

Before matrices: for vectors u, v ∈ ℝd, the inner product is ⟨u, v⟩ = Σj ujvj and the squared length is ‖u‖² = ⟨u, u⟩. With u = (1, 2) and v = (3, −1): ⟨u, v⟩ = 3 − 2 = 1 and ‖u‖² = 5. Three identities about them power whole exercise sheets:

‖u + v‖² = ‖u‖² + 2⟨u, v⟩ + ‖v‖²

(check: ‖(4, 1)‖² = 17 = 5 + 2·1 + 10 ✓) — this expansion is the single computational step of the perceptron convergence proof (Sheet 4) and the SGD stability proof (Sheet 5). Next to it sit Cauchy–Schwarz, |⟨u, v⟩| ≤ ‖u‖ ‖v‖ (here |1| ≤ √5 · √10 ≈ 7.07 ✓), which closes the perceptron mistake bound, and the triangle inequality ‖u + v‖ ≤ ‖u‖ + ‖v‖. When ⟨u, v⟩ = 0 the vectors are orthogonal and the expansion becomes Pythagoras — the geometry behind least squares.

Move 1 · multiplyMultiplication: rows dot, columns mix

The product of A (shape m × k) and B (shape k × p) exists only when the inner sizes match, and the result is m × p: entry (AB)ij is the dot product of row i of A with column j of B. For a matrix times a vector there are two equally true readings — and switching between them is half of this course:

2113 12 = 2·1 + 1·21·1 + 3·2 = 47   or   1 21 + 2 13

Rows dot: each output entry is one dot product — so (Xw)i = ⟨Xi, w⟩ is data point i's prediction. Columns mix: Xw is a linear combination of the columns of X, with weights w — so the set of all possible predictions is the column space of X. Step through a full product below.

The pictureA 2×2 matrix moves the whole plane

Feed every vector through A and you see what the matrix is. The columns tell you everything: Ae₁ is the first column, Ae₂ the second, and linearity fills in the rest — the square grid becomes a parallelogram grid. The determinant det A = ad − bc is the area factor: the unit square lands on a parallelogram of area |det A|. If det A ≠ 0 the machine can be run backwards:

A−1 = (1 / det A) d−b−ca   e.g.   2111−1 = 1−1−12

(det = 2·1 − 1·1 = 1; multiply them to check you get I, the identity — the machine that moves nothing.) If det A = 0 the plane is crushed onto a line: two different inputs land on the same output, so no inverse can exist. Play:

Move 2 · transposeTranspose: the bookkeeping move with one big rule

AT flips rows and columns: (AT)ij = Aji. An n × d matrix becomes d × n. Writing vectors as one-column matrices makes the dot product a matrix product, ⟨u, v⟩ = uTv — which is why transposes are everywhere in the sheets: they are dot products in disguise. The one rule worth memorizing is that transposing a product reverses the order:

(AB)T = BTAT   ·   (AB)−1 = B−1A−1

AB = 1201 3012 = 5412 ,  (AB)T = 5142 = BTAT ✓

Symmetric matrices

A is symmetric when AT = A. The reversal rule shows why the course's favourite matrix is always symmetric: (XTX)T = XT(XT)T = XTX. Same for XXT, for covariance matrices, and for kernel matrices — and the inverse of a symmetric matrix is symmetric too.

Moving a matrix across a dot product

⟨r, Xv⟩ = rTXv = (XTr)Tv = ⟨XTr, v⟩. The transpose hops to the other side. This one line turns “the residual is orthogonal to every prediction” into the normal equations XTr = 0 — the derivation at the heart of Sheet 6.

Move 3 · invert, safelyQuadratic forms: why XTX is special

A symmetric matrix A defines a quadratic form vTAv — a bowl, a saddle, or a flat-bottomed valley, depending on A. Call A positive definite if vTAv > 0 for every v ≠ 0 (a true bowl: one lowest point), and positive semi-definite if ≥ 0 is all you can say. The course's key example is a one-line proof:

vTXTXv = (Xv)T(Xv) = ‖Xv‖² ≥ 0

So XTX is always at least semi-definite, and the only way to hit zero is Xv = 0. If the columns of X are linearly independent (rank d), that forces v = 0 — the form is positive definite, and a positive definite matrix is invertible. That is the exact sense in which least squares “works when the features aren't redundant”. The dials of a symmetric machine are its eigenvalues: directions v where Av = λv, pure stretches with no turning. Positive definite ⟺ all λ > 0; a zero eigenvalue is a flat valley direction; a negative one bends the bowl into a saddle. And regularization is now one sentence: XTX + λI adds λ to every eigenvalue, lifting flat valleys into bowls — that is why the ridge solution exists no matter how degenerate X is.

The payoffThe formulas the course runs on

Every one of these is now readable as a chain of the moves above — multiply, transpose, invert a positive definite matrix:

Normal equations · Sheet 6

XTXw = XTy

“The residual y − Xw is orthogonal to every column of X” — least squares without calculus. The whole story is the residual explainer.

OLS & weighted · Sheets 6–7

ŵ = (XTX)−1XTy

Exists because rank-d features make XTX positive definite. Weighting points swaps in (XTRX)−1XTRy — see reading coefficients.

Ridge · Sheet 6

ŵλ = (XTX + λI)−1XTy

Stack √λ I under X as extra “phantom rows” and this is just OLS on the taller matrix — and since + λI lifts every eigenvalue, it exists for any X. The λ dial lives in regimes & ridge.

Minimum norm · Sheets 6 & 10

ŵmn = XT(XXT)−1y

The mirror world d > n: many perfect fits, and this one is the shortest. Note the pattern — when XTX (d×d) fails, XXT (n×n) steps in. It returns as the interpolator of double descent in Sheet 10.

Projection · Sheet 8

P = X(XTX)−1XT

Py is the fitted vector — y's shadow on the column space. Two signature properties: PT = P and P² = P (projecting twice changes nothing). These two lines are why boosting linear models stalls after one round.

Gram & kernel matrices · Sheets 9–10

Kij = ⟨φ(xi), φ(xj)⟩,   f(x) = k(x)TK−1y

An n × n matrix of pairwise dot products — symmetric and positive semi-definite by the same one-line proof. The late sheets live on its eigenvalues: smallest eigenvalue ⟹ invertibility, ‖K−1‖ = 1/λmin, and the covariance spectrum λ₁ ≥ λ₂ ≥ … decides whether interpolation is benign.

The bigger picture. The sheets never ask for linear algebra for its own sake — they ask you to read chains like (XTX + λI)−1XTy fluently. Fluency is three habits. Check shapes first: every product must chain (n×d)(d×1) → (n×1); half of all confusion is a shape error. Read multiplication both ways: rows when you think about data points, columns when you think about what the model can express. Trust the bowl: anything of the form XTX or K is symmetric and at least semi-definite, so it can be inverted the moment nothing is redundant — and a ridge λ buys that moment for any matrix whatsoever.
